Farmland and Timber Investments Compared With Other Real Assets

LO 20.7: Demonstrate knowledge of farmland and timber investments, as compared with other real assets investments.

For example:

  • Identify the investment characteristics of real assets.
  • Discuss the risk and return expectations for agriculture investments.
  • Discuss how the ease of investment, high liquidity, effective inflation hedging, positive portfolio diversification, and low interest rate sensitivity of agriculture investments affect their appropriateness for an institutional portfolio.

There is a considerable amount of ambiguity when it comes to categorizing an asset class as a “real” asset. Typically, to be considered a real asset, the class will have at least one of the following characteristics:

  • Long term. The asset offers long-term risk and return profiles that are appropriate for investors looking to fund their long-term liabilities.
  • Inflation hedge. The asset has a positive correlation with either U.S. or European price levels.
  • Scarce input. The asset tends to benefit from the increase in production input scarcity, primarily in economic sectors such as manufacturing, agriculture, and energy.
  • Intrinsic value. The expectation is that the asset will maintain its value during difficult financial market periods or significant changes in the economic environment due to business cycle changes.
  • Economic infrastructure. The assets represent key components to economic infrastructure, including transportation (air, roads, shipping, rail), the built environment (residential and commercial real estate), and major projects such as pipelines and telecommunications.

Investments in core exposures in timber, farmland, and agriculturally oriented infrastructure tend to have fairly modest risk and return profiles, with fairly substantial periods of potential over- and underperformance possible depending on the macroeconomic environment. As investors take on greater operating exposure in these assets, expected returns and risks may increase. Although the assumption is that risk and return move in tandem, some asset classes, such as commodities, may have higher levels of risk and lower expected returns. An investor may choose to invest in commodities even with such a risk/return profile because of the other advantages they offer, including high liquidity, positive diversification benefits, ease of investment, their role as an inflation hedge, and a low sensitivity to interest rates. For assets such as timber, farmland, and agriculturally related infrastructure, investors benefit from relatively low operating risk associated with utilizing these assets, a large portion of their asset values consisting of tangible value, and the critical role they play in many global economies. Figure 1 shows several key characteristics for a selection of real asset classes.

In order for investors to access timberland, farmland, and agricultural infrastructure, investors will typically go through managers deploying capital in private market transactions rather than through the purchase of publicly listed securities. For these three classes, liquidity and ease of investment tend to be lower than with listed securities because of the private market nature of the transactions.

Both timberland and farmland serve as valuable inflation hedges within an investment portfolio due to their long-standing positive correlation with the U.S. Consumer Price Index (CPI). Due to their exposures, which are less correlated to core beta than other assets, real assets tend to be strong portfolio diversifiers relative to portfolios with heavy concentrations in stocks and bonds. As shown in Figure 1, many real assets exhibit interest-rate sensitivity stemming from either their dependence on the financing costs of owning real assets or the capitalization of the cash flows they generate into asset values at rates that are dependent on the current interest-rate environment.
