The concept of separating alpha and beta is closely related to portable alpha. Portable alpha involves exploiting opportunities to increase alpha while simultaneously managing beta exposure to a target level.

Transferring Risk with Appropriately Sized Derivatives

It is possible to control beta while porting alpha with derivatives. For example, consider an active manager with the S&P 500 designated as the appropriate benchmark for the strategic allocation of the portfolio. She generates alpha by investing in small-cap stocks but is concerned that the systematic risk of small-cap stocks is different from the benchmark.

The goal of the active strategy is to generate alpha relative to a benchmark appropriate to the strategy. In this case, the Russell 2000 might be an appropriate benchmark for the alpha generating strategy. To align the systematic risk of the portfolio with the benchmark, she can take a short position in futures contracts on an index with characteristics similar to the small-cap securities (i.e., the Russell 2000). At the same time, she should take a long position in S&P 500 futures contracts to ensure that the portfolio has the desired exposure to the benchmark. She must determine the appropriately sized derivatives contracts to accomplish both goals.

The futures position needed to hedge is equal to the size of the position to be hedged times the beta of the position being hedge relative to the index. This is illustrated in the following equation:

futures contract’s notional value for hedging = value of the position to be hedged × beta

Portable Alpha Applications

There are several ways to separate alpha from beta. Alternative methods used to offset small-cap or other risks and layer on the systematic risk of a benchmark include:

  • Futures contracts.
  • Swap contracts.
  • Options.
  • Long and short positions in indices using exchange-traded funds (ETFs) and cash products.

When using futures to offset small-cap risk and layer on the risk of the S&P 500, the process involves:

  • Investing cash in the small-cap strategy to generate positive alpha.
  • Taking a short position in a small-cap index (e.g., the Russell 2000) using futures contracts to offset small-cap risk.
  • Taking a long position in the S&P 500 via S&P 500 futures contracts to layer on the risk exposure of large-cap stocks and bring systematic risk exposure in line with the benchmark (i.e., the S&P 500).

The return from the strategy should ideally equal the return on the benchmark plus the alpha generated from the small-cap strategy relative to the small-cap benchmark. Tracking error is likely to occur if the alpha generating strategy is not perfectly correlated with its benchmark (i.e., the Russell 2000 in this example). Tracking error increases the total risk of the portfolio beyond that of the S&P 500. However, it is expected to be idiosyncratic risk and could possibly be diversified away.

Alpha cannot be ported in all cases. For example, a manager who generates alpha by investing in private equity deals may not be able to offset the risk of the private equity sector because short-selling and derivative contacts are not readily available. Potential hedging instruments do not exist for all investment opportunities. Thus, hedging may be difficult or impossible in some cases.

Example: Calculating return of portable alpha strategy

Bob Higgins has a $100 million dollar portfolio. The portfolio benchmark is the S&P 500. The expected return on large-cap stocks is 10%. The risk-free rate is 0%. Higgins decides to invest 50% of the portfolio in the S&P 500 and the balance in a hedge fund with significant small-cap stock exposure. The alpha (i.e., return in excess of small-cap index return) generated by the hedge fund is 1.5%. The expected return on the small-cap index is 14%. The beta of the hedge fund relative to the small-cap index is 0.5 (the beta is relevant for determining the number of contracts required to hedge the small-cap risk, as discussed previously).

Higgins can use the concept of portable alpha by taking a long position in S&P 500 futures with the same exposure as the $50 million invested in the S&P 500. Higgins will sell futures on a small-cap index. This position will be the size of the small-cap exposure, adjusted for the beta. Calculate the expected total return of Higgins’ strategy.

Answer:

The goal of Higgins is to have a total of $100 million invested with a risk level that matches the S&P 500 while earning risk-free alpha from the hedge fund.

As a result, he has a 50% ($50 million) long position in the S&P 500 and a 50% ($50 million) long position in the hedge fund with small-cap exposure.

In order to match the equivalent S&P 500 return on $100 million, he establishes a $50 million long futures position in the S&P 500.

Higgins still needs to determine the small-cap beta exposure and hedge it with a short futures position on a small-cap index.

S&P 500

Position size: $50 million long position + $50 million futures position = $100 million

Percentage return: 10% (given)

Monetary return: $100 million × 10% (given) = $10 million

Hedge Fund

Position size: $50 million

Percentage return: 14% × 0.5 + 1.5% = 8.5%

Monetary return: 8.5% × $50 million = $4.25 million

Short Small-Cap Futures

Position size: $50 million × 0.5 beta = $25 million

Percentage return: –14% (given)

Monetary return: $25 million × –14% = –$3.5 million

Total Return

Monetary return: $10 million + $4.25 million + –$3.5 million = $10.75 million

Percentage return: $10.75 million / $100 million = 10.75%

Another way to look at this example is that Higgins earns the 10% S&P 500 return on the entire $100 million plus the alpha on the $50 million invested in the hedge fund.

expected total return = 10% on $100 million + 1.5% alpha on $50 million

= ($10 million + 0.75 million) / $100 million = 10.75%

Professor’s Note: The idea here, as described previously, is to earn the alpha while hedging away the risk of the alpha generating strategy (i.e., the risk of the small-cap exposure). The net result is the systematic risk of the benchmark with the return of the benchmark plus the alpha generated by active management. Tracking error will cause the total risk of the portfolio to exceed that of a pure investment in the benchmark, the S&P 500 in this case, because the correlation between the small-cap index return and the hedge fund return is not perfect. If there is no hedging instrument available due to the nature of the strategy (e.g., private equity), the risk resulting from the alpha generating strategy will be borne by the investor.

Portable Alpha Challenges

The previous examples rely on idealized conditions, such as a zero risk-free rate of interest, no transactions costs, and no dividends. These assumptions do not hold in the real world and therefore may reduce the effectiveness of portable alpha programs. We also assume that the estimation of alpha and beta are correct, which allows for the construction of an appropriate hedge.

Implementing a portable alpha program is complicated and requires the following:

  • Estimation of alpha. If alpha is not stable through time, the strategy could port a negative alpha to a passive index, resulting in underperformance relative to the benchmark. There could be a substantial difference in performance on a risk-adjusted basis.
  • Estimation of beta. If beta is incorrectly estimated or is changing through time (i.e., dynamic), the systematic risk of the strategy will be higher than expected.
  • Construction of an appropriate hedge. The appropriate strategy should hedge the beta risk of the alpha-generating strategy and take on the beta risk of the benchmark.

One approach to avoid problems with estimation is to port the alpha of a portfolio of active managers rather than that of a single manager. Another approach to port alpha is to minimize the risk of the portfolio as a whole. In this approach, all risks (including risks of the hedging positon that cannot be hedged) are considered. As a result, this risk minimization approach typically uses lower position sizes for the hedge due to its higher measured risk.
