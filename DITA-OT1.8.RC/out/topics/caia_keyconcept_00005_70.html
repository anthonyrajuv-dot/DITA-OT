LO 24.4

Investors look to indices to provide representative performance data for asset classes. Traditional equity and bond indices hold securities rather than derivatives. A commodity index uses unleveraged long-only futures contracts on commodities. Futures prices provide a more uniform method of measuring commodity performance than spot prices and ensure that each dollar invested in the index represents one dollar of commodity price exposure. An unleveraged index is fully collateralized by assuming risk-free assets are held in an amount equal to the notional value of the futures.

Newer indices (i.e., second- and third-generation indices) focus on improving index performance by increasing rebalancing frequency, using alternate roll schemes, using different weightings, and accounting for items such as momentum, term structure, and maturity. The varying methodologies used to gain a broad exposure to the commodity markets will result in varying levels of returns and volatility.
