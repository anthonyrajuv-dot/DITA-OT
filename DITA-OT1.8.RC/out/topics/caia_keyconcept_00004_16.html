The potential supply of private equity secondaries depends on the volume of primary market fundraising. Limited partner interests typically enter the secondary market with a three-to-five year lag. The conversion rate into the secondary market was approximately 6% in 2014–2015.

Regulatory changes have restricted institutional investors’ participation in private equity, causing many of these investors to sell their private equity holdings in the secondary market.

Debt financing in the secondary market may take the form of seller financing or structured financing through a special purpose vehicle.

The prevalence of large funds in the secondary market has decreased opportunities for buyers to benefit from pricing inefficiencies. These may still exist in the small to medium size segment.

General partners prefer that secondary buyers are integrated platforms rather than pure-play platforms because an integrated platform may invest in a future primary fundraising.

Fund restructurings are more complex secondary market transactions such as those that may take place at the end of a fund’s regular term to unwind remaining fund assets.
