The mean-variance model can also be modified to account for illiquidity, factor exposures, or estimation risk. Each of these adjustments is discussed below.

Illiquidity

The first adjustment to the mean-variance approach is for illiquidity. There are two kinds of illiquidity risks: market liquidity risk and funding liquidity risk. Market liquidity risk is when an investor is forced to sell an asset when there is not an active market. This often occurs with securities that are not publicly traded and that have very few buyers who are willing and able to buy the asset. Effectively, the seller will be quoting a price with no response and in turn will have to discount the asset to attract a buyer. During economic downturns, the penalty for being forced to sell an asset can be severe. Typical discounts for lack of liquidity will be even greater, and assets that were previously viewed as liquid may prove to be difficult to sell and also require a discount.

Funding liquidity risk refers to the inability of an investor to obtain financing, refinance or roll over current debt, or meet capital needs such as a private equity general partner’s capital call. In unlevered portfolios, funding liquidity risk does not exist because there are no debt obligations.

While funding liquidity risk can certainly be a factor in alternatives such as private equity investments, this text will primarily focus on market liquidity risk and how it affects the mean-variance framework via the liquidity penalty function. Factor Exposure

Often a portfolio manager’s decision to allocate less capital to a particular asset class is based on a specific factor. For example, a rise in interest rates will cause the fixed-income asset class to drop in value, so a manager will want to limit the amount of the portfolio allocated to bonds. Estimation Risk

Forecasting future returns requires estimation. Historical data provides a solid starting point for these estimates, but in the case of alternative investments, historical data is severely limited compared to publicly available information, and even when there is historical data to be analyzed, it rarely spans more than a few years. This makes estimations of portfolio returns naturally risky, and an adjustment for this uncertainty should be considered. Estimation risk refers to the risk that the actual parameters differ from the estimates used in a model.
