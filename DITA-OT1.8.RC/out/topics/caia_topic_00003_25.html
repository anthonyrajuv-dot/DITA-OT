Topic 3.6: Real Estate Equity Investments

The value of the underlying real estate property less the mortgage claims is the value of a real estate equity investment.

Real Estate Development Projects

Stages of the real estate development process include:

Stage 1: Acquiring the land or the site of the development project.

Stage 2: Forecasting the revenues, costs, and profitability of a planned project.

Stage 3: Designing the building. This step may include hiring architects, builders, and other professionals.

Stage 4: Securing approval through government building permits and public support of the project.

Stage 5: Raising capital to finance the project.

Stage 6: Building the structure.

Stage 7: Leasing, managing, and possibly selling the property.

Real estate development projects can be viewed as a string of real options. Real options in real estate include:

  • Call options to buy real estate.
  • Put options to sell real estate.
  • Exchange options involving the exchange of noncash assets.

Each of the following project stages can be viewed as a call option:

Stage 1: Feasibility study. Assess the potential profitability of a planned development project.

Stage 2: Purchase property. Following a favorable feasibility study, purchase suitable property.

Stage 3: Construction. Construct building(s).

Real Estate Valuation Approaches and Risk Assessment

There are several approaches used to value real estate equity investments including decision trees, which are described above. Other models include:

  • Discounted cash flow approach (or income approach). Cash flows are discounted back to the present and a comparison is made between the present value costs of the project versus the present value benefits.

To calculate the value of a real estate property, we need three components: net operating income (NOI), discount rate (k), and net sale proceeds (NSP).

  • Comparable sales approach. A DCF approach to valuation may not make sense in all cases (e.g., investment in a single family house). Data regarding the sales prices of comparable assets in the area are examined in this approach. This works best if properties are similar and there are several recent sales.
  • Profit approach. Used if the analyst is valuing the business rather than the property.
  • Equity residual approach involves subtracting interest expense and other cash flows owed to mortgage holders and then discounting the remaining cash flows to estimate the value of the project’s equity.

Alternative Real Estate Investment Vehicles

Alternative real estate investment vehicles include:

  • Private equity real estate funds invest pooled investor capital in private real estate and include commingled real estate funds, syndications, and joint ventures.
  • Commingled real estate funds (CREFs) are a specific type of private equity real estate funds that consist of privately placed commingled (i.e., pooled) capital that is invested in real estate.
  • Syndications are financing mechanisms that allow investors to raise capital and hire an expert with the intention of undertaking a real estate venture. They may acquire, develop, operate, or manage property.
  • Real estate joint ventures are formed between two or more parties who wish to undertake a real estate project.
  • Real estate limited partnerships combine the benefits of partnerships, including tax (i.e., no corporate taxation) and income distribution benefits, with the limited liability feature of a corporate investment.
  • Open-end real estate mutual funds sell shares to stockholders to raise capital and then invest this capital in real estate assets.
  • Real estate derivatives (i.e., options and futures) provide alternative mechanisms for investors to gain exposure to real estate risk and returns without having to buy or sell the underlying assets (i.e., the real estate).
  • Exchange-traded funds (ETFs) are tradable investment securities that track a particular real estate index. The ETF holds the assets, or a sub-sample of the assets, included in the index.
  • Closed-end real estate mutual funds are exchange-traded mutual funds. In addition to the advantages listed below, closed-end funds and ETFs can typically be purchased with margin and investors can take short positions.
  • Equity REITs must have 75% or more of the underlying real estate holdings representing equity claims on real estate rather than mortgage claims.

The advantages and disadvantages of alternative real estate investment vehicles are generally similar across the various types of funds.

Advantages include:

  • Access to private real estate. This is important to small institutional investors who want exposure to real estate, as well as large institutional investors who want to limit exposure to the property-specific risks of a single property.
  • Access to fund managers with specialized knowledge and expertise.
  • Some limited partnerships use leverage to enhance returns.

Disadvantages include:

  • Investors lose direct control over portfolio decisions including selling, leveraging, leasing, and strategic portfolio decisions.
  • Lack of liquidity. Some investments, such as private equity investments, are often purchased and held to liquidation.
  • Performance is difficult to measure due to potentially incorrect underlying asset valuations.
  • Some investment types are unavailable to individual investors due to high capital requirements.
  • The use of leverage in some types of funds may increase risk.

Real Estate Depreciation

Depreciation is the reduction in the value of an asset with the passage of time. Depreciation can be the actual wear and tear on the asset or the accounting value that represents the decline in value. Depreciation is a noncash expense. The tax savings that results from depreciation can have a substantial effect on after-tax cash flows. The tax savings is known as the depreciation tax shield.

The depreciation tax shield, and thus the internal rate of return (IRR) of the investment, depends on the way depreciation is handled. Examples include:

  • Depreciation (actual wear and tear) without taxation. In this case, the IRR is higher than depreciation with taxation simply because the firm does not have to pay taxes. It is a before-tax IRR.
  • Taxation without allowable depreciation. This generally results in the lowest IRR because depreciation is not allowed as a tax-deductible expense, increasing taxes and decreasing cash flow. The first principle of depreciation is that when depreciation is not allowed as a tax-deductible expense (or when the accounting depreciation rate is slower than the true economic depreciation rate), the after-tax IRR will be less than the pretax IRR reduced by the tax rate.
  • Allowable depreciation and taxation. In this case, the IRR is approximately (1 – tax rate) of the before tax (i.e., no-tax) example. For example, if the no-tax IRR is 10% and the tax rate is 40%, the IRR in this case would be approximately 6%. The effective tax rate is equal to the stated tax rate when tax-deductible depreciation equals economic depreciation. This is the second principle of depreciation.
  • Accelerated depreciation and taxation. In this case, the after-tax IRR is slightly higher than in the non-accelerated example because the tax shield is realized sooner. This is the third principle of depreciation.
  • Fully expensing the asset with taxation. In this case, the IRR is equal to the no-tax example. This illustrates the fourth principle of depreciation, when outlays can be fully expensed for tax accounting purposes, the after-tax return will be approximately equal to the before-tax return.

Real Estate Equity Risks and Returns

There are three basic approaches to computing real estate indices including:

  1. Appraisals. Prices are professionally estimated rather than generated from purchases and sales (i.e., market prices). Appraised values introduce the potential for data smoothing. The NCREIF Property Index (NPI) is based on appraised values.
  2. Adjusted privately traded prices. Prices of properties that have been sold are used to estimate hypothetical selling prices of properties that have not sold. A hedonic price index uses the underlying characteristics of an asset to estimate prices of the assets in the index.
  3. Market prices. REIT returns are based on market prices, unlike private real estate. The S&P U.S. REIT Composite Index, the Dow Jones Wilshire Real Estate Investment Trust (DJW REIT), the Real Estate Securities Index (RESI), the Real Estate Investment Trusts Index (REIT), and the MSCI U.S. REIT Index are all widely used to measure real estate returns.

Using appraised values and private transaction-based methodologies leads to data smoothing for private real estate returns. This results in artificially low volatility of prices and returns. Also, correlation with other assets is underestimated. On the other hand, REIT returns, which rely on frequent market prices, appear to be more highly correlated with the stock market than with the returns of the underlying assets (i.e., properties).

Equity REITs: Performance and Implications

The following observations regarding equity REIT performance from 2000 to 2014 are key:

  • Returns averaged a strong 14.6% over the sample period, higher than any other asset class listed.
  • The standard deviation of returns was also higher than any other listed asset class, with the exception of commodities. This implies high risk and high rewards.
  • The minimum return was –31.7% and the maximum drawdown was –68.3%.
  • Like mortgage REITs, the data also indicates negative skewness and leptokurtosis.
  • Date includes periods of extremes so these results should not necessarily be expected in the future.
  • Correlation results indicate high positive correlation with global equity and high-yield bond markets, modest positive correlation with global bonds, low positive correlation with commodities, and negative correlation with the VIX.
