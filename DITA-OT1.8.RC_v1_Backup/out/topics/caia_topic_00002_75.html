A simple option has two characteristics:

  • Value of option payoff is determined by the value of an underlying asset on a single expiration date.
  • The payoff increases linearly to the distance between the strike price and the value of the underlying asset.

An exotic option is any option that has features that are not included in a simple option (e.g., payoffs prior to expiration date and nonlinear payoffs).

A principal-protected structured product is designed to have a minimum guaranteed payout, usually equal to the investor’s principal.

The participation rate describes how much a product’s payout increases as the value of the underlying asset changes.

In a cash-and-call strategy, the investor owns a long position in cash or a zero-coupon bond and a long call. Put-call parity requires that the payout of this position equals that of a protective put.

A path-dependent option is valued based on the price of the underlying asset at points up to and including the expiration date. Five key types of path-dependent options are:

  • Binary option: has two outcomes depending on the price of the underlying asset relative to a specific threshold at expiration.
  • Barrier option: has a range of payoffs and a change in payoff if the underlying asset reaches a specific level during a specific time period. When this occurs, the option becomes an active option. For each barrier and option type, there are four potential combinations as seen below:

  • Spread option: payoff is the difference between two prices.
  • Look-back option: payoff is based on either the largest or the smallest difference between the two reference prices over the life of the option.
  • Quanto option: has a payoff in one currency using the numerical value of the underlying asset expressed in a different currency.
