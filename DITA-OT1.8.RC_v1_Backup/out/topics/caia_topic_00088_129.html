Equity and Debt Values

A firm’s assets (A) are simply equal the sum of its debt (D) and equity (E) where At = Dt + Et. At maturity, the value of the equity and the payoff to bondholders at time T with a face value of K is:

ET = (AT − K, 0)
DT = K – max(K – AT, 0)

Advantages and Disadvantages of the Merton Model

Despite its restrictive assumptions and lack of applicability to real world scenarios, the Merton model is very useful as an initial assessment before looking at more complex models. The model does not look at the true probability of default, but instead looks at the probability implied by market prices determined by risk-neutral investors.

The model suffers from several shortcomings. Many of the model parameters are not readily observable, including the return volatility and the asset market values. The model also does not explain the credit spreads on short-term securities well.

Four Important Properties of the Merton Model

The Merton model has four important properties:

  1. Sensitivity to maturity. As maturity increases, the probability of default rises. When maturity is extended, default probabilities continue to rise, but at a decreasing rate. Credit spreads initially increase with maturity, but then begin to decline as maturity further increases. This is because risky debt is essentially a risk-free debt combined with a short position in a European put option.
  2. If the date of optimal exercise comes before maturity, the put option value could begin to decline since the owner has to delay exercising an in-the-money option.
  3. Sensitivity to asset volatility. As asset volatility increases, so does the probability of default. At the same time, the credit spread also increases, although by less for long-term bonds relative to short-term bonds. This is because the fluctuation in asset values is asymmetrical because it is bound by zero as a floor, but is unlimited on the upside.
  4. Sensitivity to leverage. Higher leverage increases the probability of default and credit spread because the use of leverage is also asymmetrical.
  5. Sensitivity to the risk-free rate. The Merton model assumes that the expected rate of return on assets is equal to the risk-free rate plus a risk premium. As the risk-free rate increases, so does the return expectation, reducing default probability and credit spreads.
