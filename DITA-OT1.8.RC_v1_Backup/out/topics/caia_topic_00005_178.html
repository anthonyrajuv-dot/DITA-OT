Allocation Considerations and Operational Decision Making

LO 33.7: Demonstrate knowledge of operational decision making and allocation considerations.

For example:

  • Discuss common operational decisions and allocation conclusions that result from the ODD process.

At the conclusion of an ODD review, an investor makes an operational decision, also known as operational risk determination. If the investor determines that the operational risk of the fund is unacceptably high, the investor will not invest in the fund or may reevaluate the fund at a later date if the fundâ€™s deficiencies have been fixed. Conversely, an investor would make an investment allocation to the fund if the operational risk was determined to be sufficiently low.

Some investors use factor weightings based on the importance of each operational factor and then calculate an overall risk score for each fund. Other investors might employ the use of operational threshold issues, which are binary requirements for investing in a fund. For example, some investors will not consider an investment in a fund that does not use a top-tier accounting firm or one that self-administers its own funds.

Comparing operational best practices to existing procedures at a fund is known as operational benchmarking. Determining the best operational practices to follow is a quantitative and qualitative pursuit, which will vary by strategy and the instruments that are traded. It is considered a best practice to use a qualitative approach with limited quantitative inputs (i.e., information-based approach) rather than relying on the pure quantitative approach used in risk-scoring models.
