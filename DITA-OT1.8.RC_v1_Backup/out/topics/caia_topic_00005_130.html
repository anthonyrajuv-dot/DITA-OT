Characteristics of the KMV Model

The Kealhover, McQuown, and Vasicek (KMV) credit risk model is a structural credit risk model. It is built on the Merton model, but also uses estimates of the volatility and value of a firm’s assets and equity to estimate the credit risk of debt. Instead of looking at credit risk from a lender’s perspective, it is used to analyze the viewpoint of the equity holder. Equity market value can be observed in the market for traded firms, and equity return volatility can be observed from historical returns. Once Et and E are observed, At and A can also be observed.

One important difference between the KMV and the Merton models is the treatment of default. While both models use a default trigger (i.e., event or condition that causes default), the calculation of the trigger differs. The KMV model uses a weighted average of short-term and long-term bonds, and therefore default is calculated only when the asset values fall below the combined value of the short-term and long-term debt. By contrast, in the Merton model the default trigger for the firm’s asset value is the face value of the zero-coupon bond, because it is related to the asset value the firm would have at default. If the asset value declines below the face value of the bond, the model assumes the firm is in default.
