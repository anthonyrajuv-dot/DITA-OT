The theory of storage states that investors derive benefits from holding physical commodities rather than only investing in futures and forward contracts. Convenience yield is the non-monetary benefit from holding physical commodity. Convenience yield is inversely related to the level of inventories. Consumer surplus refers to the difference between the market price of a commodity and the price a firm would be willing to pay for it.

The convenience yield is higher with low inventory levels, higher prices and higher volatility, and declining futures prices.

Cost of carry is the measure of storage costs for a commodity and is calculated as:

cost of carry = financing costs + storage costs + spoilage costs − convenience yield

An investor can conduct cash-and-carry arbitrage by purchasing the commodity in the spot market, selling an overpriced futures contract, and delivering the commodity upon contract expiration. Cash-and-carry arbitrage refers to the simultaneous purchase of a commodity in the spot market and its sale in the futures market to earn a risk-free profit. If the futures price is below the breakeven price, the transaction is referred to as a reverse cash-and-carry arbitrage.

The formula for the breakeven futures price is as follows:

breakeven futures price = spot price + cost of carry – convenience yield
