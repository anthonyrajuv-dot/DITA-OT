Topic 3.1: Asset Allocation Processes and the Mean-Variance Model

Importance of Asset Allocation

Asset allocation refers to two concepts:

  1. The steps taken in determining the proper asset classes for an investor.
  2. The breakdown of the chosen portfolio asset classes given the investor’s objectives and constraints.

Security selection largely determines return in a concentrated portfolio but has minimal impact if the portfolio is well diversified. A 1986 study of U.S. pension funds found that 90% of return variability was determined by asset allocation and 10% was due to active management. This study (confirmed by others) concluded:

  1. Variability of returns. The asset allocation of portfolios of diversified, traditional assets determines 85% to 90% of the volatility of returns.
  2. Returns of portfolios with different asset allocations. Less than half of the differences in returns of portfolios can be attributed to differing allocations to asset classes, with the remaining differences being due to factors such as style, timing, security selection, and fees.
  3. Impact of asset allocation on average return. Active management has little impact on portfolio return, as 65% to 85% of mutual and pension funds have underperformed their passive benchmarks.

Five Steps of the Asset Allocation Process

A systematic asset allocation approach involves:

Step 1: Determine the asset owner and identify their objectives and constraints.

Step 2: Specify the asset allocation approach. This includes the creation of the investment policy statement (IPS). Important parts of the IPS include objectives and constraints, the different asset classes that will be considered for investment, the choice of active or passive management, and when and how the allocation can be amended.

Step 3: Implement the asset allocation determined by the IPS. This includes calculating the weights of the portfolio’s asset classes.

Step 4: Allocate capital. This step involves allocating the capital to the necessary asset class weightings.

Step 5: Monitor and evaluate investments.

Asset Owners

Asset owners include endowments and foundations, pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and family offices. Each have objectives and constraints that shape the asset allocation. For example, endowments have very long investment horizons and are thus not limited in terms of the asset classes in which they may invest.

Investment Policy Objectives

The objectives of asset owners must be stated in terms of risk and return. Investors may focus on:

  • Expected returns and standard deviation of returns.
  • Expected utility. Utility is defined as the fulfillment an investor receives from an investment’s return. The probability-weighted average of the utility of all possible investment outcomes is the expected utility. An investor prefers the asset with the greater expected utility. A utility function is used to represent an investment’s outcome to the investor’s utility. Assume investment A has an equal chance to increase by 6% or decrease by 4%. The utility function is:
E[U(WA)] = 0.5 × U(W × 1.06) + 0.5 × U(W × 0.96)

Investment Policy Constraints

The internal constraints of asset owners may include liquidity, time horizon, and sector and country limits. The external constraints are created by forces outside of the investor’s control and may include tax status and regulations.

Components of an IPS

The seven common components of an IPS are:

  1. Background.
  2. Objectives—stating the owner’s goals.
  3. Asset classes—types of asset classes that can be considered and which assets will use passive or active approaches.
  4. Governance.
  5. Manager selection.
  6. Reporting and monitoring.
  7. Strategic asset allocation (SAA).

Strategic Asset Allocation: Risk and Return

Strategic asset allocation (SAA) provides a long-term asset allocation goal for the fund and may state upper and lower bounds for asset allocations. SAA allows for the tactical asset allocation (TAA) to capitalize on short-term opportunities such as strong sectors or short-term market inefficiencies.

All asset classes have an expected return consisting of:

asset class return = short-term real riskless rate + expected inflation + risk premium

Return estimates are more difficult for alternative assets because:

  • There is a lack of robust historical data.
  • Alpha is a source of return. Alpha is not unlimited, and as more investment is made into alternative asset classes, the additional competition will decrease alpha.
  • Alternative investment classes as a whole are constantly changing.

MVO With Liabilities, Multiple Risky Assets, and Hurdle Rates

The MVO equation can be used to solve the problem of tracking error related to assets and liabilities.

Given multiple risky assets, all the optimal combinations of return and standard deviations based on asset class weights for risk-averse investors can be used to create the efficient frontier. No portfolios can be formed with the same return but lower standard deviations (or the same standard deviation but higher returns) than those found on the efficient frontier.

A hurdle rate is defined as the return that the new asset must have to be added to an optimal portfolio. The inclusion of a new asset to a portfolio that is already optimal will increase expected utility if the return on the asset is higher than the hurdle rate. Adding the asset will, in effect, shift the efficient frontier up and to the left (i.e., resulting in a “more efficient” efficient frontier).

Data Issues for Illiquid Assets and Large-Scale Optimization

Alternative assets:

  • Are often illiquid.
  • Lack higher frequency data (sometimes only quarterly, smoothed data available).
  • Have smoothed return data, which minimizes variances and covariances.
  • Are low volatility and low correlation with other assets (that may result from smoothed data) investments. As such, they are given large weights (i.e., over-weighted) in the optimization process.

When the group of possible investments gets very large, problems with estimating covariance begin to arise (e.g., nearly 80,000 covariances for a 400 asset optimization). Investors use factor models, which use a small amount of factors to compare to the stocks, as an alternative to MVO.

As mean-variance optimization relies on the mean and variance or returns for inputs, it can be very sensitive to changes to the mean return and covariance matrix. The Black-Litterman approach was created to curtail the unreasonable weights given by MVO based on the manager’s mean and variance estimates.
