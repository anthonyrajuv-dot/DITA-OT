The following are key elements characterizing hedge funds:

  • Hedge funds are privately organized and generally unlisted.
  • Hedge funds offer managers performance-based fees.
  • Hedge funds invest in private securities, real assets, derivatives, and structured products and apply leverage, short sell securities, and take concentrated positions.

Relative to mutual funds, hedge funds can be managed with more flexibility in the following six ways:

  1. Invest in private securities and real assets.
  2. Use leverage, sometimes in high amounts.
  3. Use derivatives, including long or short futures positions and buying or writing options.
  4. Sell securities short.
  5. Invest in structured products and other nontraditional securities.
  6. Use complex active management strategies and dynamic risk exposures

The long-term growth trend for hedge funds is the result of their favorable investment attributes, which include:

  • Low correlations with traditional investments, which provide diversification benefits to investors.
  • The ability of hedge funds to hold both long and short positions.
  • The potential for larger returns than traditional investments provide.

The 2007 financial crisis sparked a consolidation trend in the hedge fund industry as smaller participants exited the market and large firms became even larger.
