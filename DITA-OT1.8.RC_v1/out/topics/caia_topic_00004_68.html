Risk budgeting is the process of selecting a maximum amount of desired total risk for a portfolio and allocating the portfolio in such a way that the combined risks of the portfolio’s individual asset classes do not exceed the selected limit. Risk can be measured in several ways, but the most common measurements include standard deviations of returns, standard deviation of tracking error against a benchmark, value at risk, and beta. As can be seen, risk can be measured in either absolute terms (standard deviation of returns and value at risk) or in relative terms (tracking error and beta).

Risk Budgeting Using Factors

Similar to measuring the total risk of a portfolio through the contributions of its individual asset classes, the same method can be used with risk factors. Linear regression allows an analyst to measure the effects of any number of risk factors for a portfolio. For a portfolio that has two primary risk factors, the regression model is as follows:

RPt = a + b1F1t + b2F2t + t

Rp is the rate of return for the portfolio, a is the intercept of the regression, b1 and b2 are the estimated effects on the portfolio from factors F1 and F2, respectively, and  represents the residual return of the regression that is not explained by either of the factors. From here, it is possible to separate the effect each risk factor has on the portfolio:

P = (F1 × F1 × b1) + (F2 × F2 × b2) + ( × )

F1 and F2 represent the correlations of the two factors with the return of the portfolio, and  is the correlation of the residual risk with the return of the portfolio. The segments of this equation represent the individual risk contributions of the factors (both known and unknown) and therefore simply need to be added together to determine the portfolio’s total risk.

Example: Risk budgeting

Portfolio A has a total risk of 9%. If the correlation between changes in interest rates and the return on Portfolio A is 0.26, the standard deviation of interest rate changes is 14%, and the estimated factor effect (coefficient) on the portfolio from interest rates is 0.82, how much does the change in interest rates contribute to the portfolio’s total risk?

Answer:

interest rate contribution = 0.26 × 0.14 × 0.82 = 2.98%

Changes in interest rates account for 2.98%, or approximately one-third, of the portfolio’s total risk of 9%.
