Four Core Decisions of a Futures Trading System

Futures trading is different from regular investing because the former involves taking long or short positions instead of actually holding the underlying assets. There are four core decisions to consider:

  1. Entry. When should a position be taken?
  2. Size. What is the appropriate size for the position?
  3. Exit. When should the investor unwind the position?
  4. Allocation. What is the appropriate risk or capital to allocate to various sectors and markets?

Use of Data Processing in Futures Portfolio Construction

The information used in portfolio construction could be both technical and fundamental in nature. From a pricing perspective, there is a key issue with the rolling forward of contracts from expiring to new ones. Rolling forward creates pricing gaps, and as a result, necessitates pricing adjustments near contract expiration dates. Removing such gaps would allow for continuous pricing of futures contracts.

Market Allocation

Market allocations comprise risk and capital components. With respect to updated allocations based on risk, the risk loading and volatility adjustments described in the previous equations are relevant. Capital allocations to individual markets are dependent on the allocated risk and the amount of realized volatility in each market. As a result, the notional exposure in a given market could be extremely high if the market’s volatility is the same. In other words, the risk allocation and the notional exposures are not necessarily the same.

There are three common methods of risk allocation:

  1. Equal dollar risk allocation. The same dollar amount is invested in each market, and there is no regard for any correlation between markets.
  2. Equal risk contribution. This considers the risk contribution of market and considers correlation between markets.
  3. Market capacity weighting. This depends on individual market capacity, or market size, based on daily trading volume and price volatility.

Because CTA funds with a greater amount of assets are unable to allocate capital to markets with lower open interest and volumes, risk allocations methods for such funds tend to be similar to market capacity weighting.

Trading Execution

There are a wide variety of ways to implement trading signals into actual positions. To the extent that execution is delayed for short-term strategies, execution speed and alpha decay become relevant. Alpha decay refers to the rate in which performance diminishes when execution is delayed. For long-term (trend-following) strategies, cost is the relevant consideration. Therefore, trend-following strategies do not require the fastest futures trading systems and can divide daily orders into numerous intraday orders to get a better sense of any price trends. To minimize costs, market orders are most frequently used.
