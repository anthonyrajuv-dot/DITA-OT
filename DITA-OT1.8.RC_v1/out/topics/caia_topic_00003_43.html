Topic 6.4: Equity-Linked Structured Products

Equity-linked structured products are based on low risk assets guaranteeing a minimum level of return and then provide additional upside by tying returns to a risky asset. They differ from other structured product types that are based on pools of risky assets.

Wrappers and Tax Effects

A wrapper is a legal contract between the issuer and investor that lays out specific terms of the equity-linked structured product. Wrappers usually have some benefit to investors such as reduced taxes or default protection.

Six types of wrappers are:

  1. Over-the-counter (OTC) contracts.
  2. Medium-term notes/certificates/warrants.
  3. Funds.
  4. Life insurance policies.
  5. Structured deposits.
  6. Islamic wrappers.

Exotic Options

An exotic option is any option that has features that are not included in a simple option (e.g., payoffs prior to expiration date and nonlinear payoffs). Common exotic options include binary options, Asian options, spread options, look-back options, quanto options, and barrier options.

  • Binary option: has two outcomes depending on the price of the underlying asset relative to a specific threshold at expiration.
  • Asian option: has payoff profiles based on the average price of the security over the life of the option. An Asian option is a considered a path-dependent option. A path-dependent option is valued based on the price of the underlying asset at points up to and including the expiration date.
  • Spread option: payoff is the difference between two prices.
  • Look-back option: payoff is based on either the largest or the smallest difference between the two reference prices over the life of the option.
  • Quanto option: has a payoff in one currency using the numerical value of the underlying asset expressed in a different currency.
  • Barrier option: has a range of payoffs and a change in payoff if the underlying asset reaches a specific level during a specific time period. When this occurs, the option becomes an active option. A knock-in option is a barrier option that becomes effective if the underlying asset reaches a specific level at any point before expiration. In a knock-out option, the underlying put or call option terminates if the underlying asset reaches a particular price. Global Structured Products

Simplified examples of global structured products include:

  • U.S.-based structured product with multiple kinks = underlying asset + bear put spread − out-of-the-money call
  • French-based structured product with floors = underlying portfolio + portfolio of bear spreads + portfolio of short binary puts
  • German-based structured product with leverage = underlying asset + bull spread
  • U.K.-based absolute-return structured product = at-the-money up-and-out call + at-the-money down-and-out put
  • Swiss-based structured product based on absolute returns = at-the-money call + 2 at-the-money down-and-out puts − at-the-money put
  • Japan-based structured product based on multiple currencies = foreign fixed interest rate − domestic fixed interest rate

Liquid structured products are listed on major market exchanges. A disadvantage is that listed structured products may fail to meet an investor’s particular needs due to the products’ standardized nature. However, these products offer investors better monitoring and improved access.

Structured Products Pricing

Methods for pricing structured products include:

  • Partial differential equation (PDE) approach. This method assumes the value of a structured product can be determined by analyzing and predicting the movement of the underlying asset and then formulating a hedge based on the structured product and its underlying asset. It establishes solutions based on boundary conditions and uses dynamic hedging.
  • Monte Carlo simulation approach. This approach uses hypothetical paths for the underlying asset’s value to create an estimate for how the structured product would pay out under each situation. This approach offers simplicity and is able to provide estimated values for complex structured products that are unable to utilize the PDE approach.
  • Building blocks or portfolio approach. This method breaks down a structured product into easier to analyze assets, estimates the value of each of the pieces, and then sums the values to determine the total value of product. This approach uses static hedging.

Payoff Diagrams of Structured Products

When investors look at payoff diagrams, they look at two aspects:

  • The payoff diagram shape shows risk exposure of the structured product compared to an underlying asset. Investors look at the shape to determine if the risk exposures meet their investment preferences. Any payoff diagram shape can be constructed as long as there are adequate types and numbers of options with which to manage the risk exposure.
  • The payoff diagram level tells investors what return (percentage or amount) they can expect, relative to the price of the product. Investors look at the level to decide whether the product is attractively priced. The level of the payoff diagram determines whether the product is priced appropriately.

Some studies suggest that structured products are often overpriced at issue. There is also evidence that total fees and expenses in some cases are lower than empirical studies suggest.

Motivation of Investors and Issuers

Motivations for investing in structured products:

  1. Enhanced returns.
  2. Enhanced liquidity.
  3. Improved risk management.
  4. Improved diversification.
  5. Improved access to top managers.
  6. Regulatory advantages.
  7. Reduced income taxes.
  8. Reduced transaction taxes.

Motivations for issuing structured products:

  1. Fee revenue and profitability.
  2. Beneficial source of financing (i.e., lower financing costs, superior risk exposures, desirable maturities).
