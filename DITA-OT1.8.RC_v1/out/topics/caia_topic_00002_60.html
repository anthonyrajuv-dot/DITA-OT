LO 26.3

Credit derivatives have the following three major economic roles:

  1. Allow a bank to continue making loans to a major client by providing a mechanism to transfer some of the risk of the loans to another party.
  2. Act to provide liquidity to the market in times of financial market stress. Credit derivatives have changed credit risk from an illiquid risk to a liquid risk that can be traded like any other.
  3. Assist in price discovery. The highly-liquid markets for credit derivatives provide information about what informed buyers and sellers think various securities are worth.

Credit derivatives can be grouped into three major categories:

  1. Single-name versus multiname. Single-name instruments reference just one entity, while multiname instruments reference a number of securities.
  2. Funded versus unfunded. Funded credit instruments involve the transfer of notional principal, while unfunded instruments do not.
  3. Non-sovereign versus sovereign. Sovereign instruments such as government bonds are more complex to model than non-sovereign instruments such as corporate bonds, because sovereign instruments are exposed to additional risks such as willingness to pay.

There have been four major stages of credit derivatives:

  • Stage 1: Defensive Stage (late 1980s–early 1990s).
  • Stage 2: Emergence of Intermediaries (1991–late 1990s).
  • Stage 3: Development of Regulations (late 1990s–2002).
  • Stage 4: Liquid Market (2003–present).
