Minor and Major Renovations

  1. Conditions: Minor and Major Renovations
    1. Condition
      1. Minor renovation
        1. Almost every buy-fix-sell property has at least one significant problem—often the result of deferred maintenance.
        2. A minor renovation is a property that needs a little bit of significant work, in addition to cosmetics.
        3. To remain a minor renovation (versus a major renovation), a buy-fix-sell property would need only one or a few problems fixed.
        4. These are the type of items that may warrant a repair or replace (R & R) designation on municipal or private home inspections.
        5. These improvements generally cost more than they add to value, so be sure to buy the property accordingly.
        6. You make your money when you buy. Make sure the acquisition price of your buy-fix-sell property reflects the major problem.
        7. The following are examples of significant problems, which are relatively minor if only one or two items need to be addressed:
          1. Chimney repair (usually tuck point)
          2. New roof
            1. Re-shingle
            2. Re-roof
            3. Re-deck
          3. Soffit and fascia
          4. Gutter and downspout
          5. Exterior trim
          6. Exterior doors
          7. Storm windows
          8. Windows (a few)
          9. Full exterior paint
          10. Siding repair or replacement
          11. Tuck point foundation walls
          12. New front or rear porch stoops
          13. Replace small portions of some interior walls
          14. Ceramic tile work
          15. Tear out and replace tub surround
          16. Replace interior doors
          17. Update electrical panel
          18. Replace plumbing fixtures, water heater
          19. Replace forced air furnace or central air conditioner
          20. Finish basement/attic (really a major project)
      2. Major renovation (gut rehab)
        1. If a property needs several of the aforementioned improvements, it is considered a major renovation.
          1. These properties often need to be brought up to code.
          2. These properties may have several municipal work orders and code violations.
          3. Condemned properties and some foreclosures often need major rehab.
        2. The following are often a major renovation:
          1. Water-damaged buildings
          2. Storm-damaged buildings
          3. Fire-damaged buildings
          4. Buildings inundated with mold
        3. Sometimes, a buy-fix-sell property has previously been bought, fixed, and sold, but the improvements were substandard.
          1. Restoration can be a major rehab.
          2. Symptoms include the following:
            1. Removal of the original trim
            2. Paneling applied to walls
            3. Lowered ceilings
            4. Installation of low acoustical ceilings
            5. Sloppy painting
            6. Installation of cheap doors, windows, cabinets, and plumbing fixtures
        4. Restoring a single-family home that has been unprofessionally chopped up into a duplex, triplex, or multi-unit apartment.
        5. Proceed with caution on a major rehab; these buy-fix-sell properties are only for the experienced builder, remodeler, or developer.
        6. These improvements generally cost more than they add to value, so be sure to buy the property accordingly.
        7.  Remember: You make your money when you buy.
        8. A major renovation would include several of the following:
          1. Replace most or all interior drywall or plaster walls (gut rehab)
          2. Structural work
          3. Sagging roof
          4. Sagging floors and ceilings
          5. Foundation wall
          6. Redesign floor plan
          7. Rewiring (usually requires at least partial demolition of walls or ceilings)
          8. Remove and replace plumbing supply, waste, and vent
          9. Replace a boiler (hot water or steam heat)
          10. Remove old, asbestos-clad heating units
          11. Rebuild front- or rear-covered porch
          12. New garage
          13. Replace all or most windows and exterior doors
  2. SOURCING PROPERTY
    1. Networking
      1. Once you have determined where to look, you need to contact whoever may need to know that you are buying property.
        1. Successful renovators know that sourcing property is the key to their business, so they often create a marketing program to locate property.
        2. Instead of looking for property, they want property owners to contact them.
      2. Drive through neighborhoods of interest, check county records, and contact owners of particular properties
      3. Network with other investors
        1. Call FSBOs selling fixer-upper or rental property via classified ads
      4. Wholesalers—individuals who purchase multiple properties for resale
        1. May offer seller financing
      5. Real estate agents/brokers
        1. Network with agents/brokers
        2. Some agents/brokers specialize in buy-fix-sell, and many specialize in short sales and foreclosed property
      6. Mail cards to property owners in your market area
      7. Mail cards to out-of-town owners
      8. Contact owners of rental property with chronic vacancy signs
      9. Distribute flyers in your target area
      10. Billboards, bus benches
      11. Give everyone you meet your business card
        1.  I buy property—any location, any condition
      12. Multiple listing service (MLS)
      13. Preforeclosures (short sales)
      14. Foreclosure auctions
      15. REO (real estate owned) foreclosing lenders
    2. Lender workout options for sellers
      1. Mortgage modification requires application
        1. Tax forms, income and expense statements, hardship letter
        2. May require intermediary mortgage counselor
      2. Options for owner
        1. Repay delinquent payments
        2. Loan modification
        3. Short sale
        4. Deed in lieu of foreclosure
        5. Foreclosure
      3. Buying short-sale properties
        1. A short sale occurs when a seller attempts to sell a property for an amount that is less than the mortgage(s) owed on the property.
        2. The lender(s) must approve a short sale.
          1. Seller markets property
          2. Seller accepts an offer subject to lender approval
          3. Lender’s approval/disapproval can take days, weeks, or months
          4. Work with agents with short-sale experience
          5. Title will be as is and may be encumbered by liens
            1. Second and third mortgages
            2. Property tax
            3. Water, sewer, trash
    3. Buying foreclosed properties
      1. Buying at the sheriff’s sale
        1. Typically need certified funds
        2. Statutory redemption period (varies from state to state)
        3. Sales conducted through a government agency
        4. Lender is often the high bidder
        5. Lender receives a sheriff’s certificate
        6. Statutory redemption period begins after foreclosure sale
        7. Property owner can maintain use and control of property throughout the statutory redemption period
      2. Buying REO properties
        1. Lender, lender’s agent (or buyer at sheriff’s sale) sells the property after the statutory redemption period
        2. All liens generally removed
        3. Check municipality for vacant, boarded, and condemned list
          1. May be fees and work orders associated with vacant property
        4. Make offer attractive to lender
          1. Fewer contingencies
            1. Financing
            2. Inspection
          2. Lenders may accept cash offers over higher contingent offers
      3. Lender often requires a lender-specific PA
      4. Lender property disclosure waivers
      5. As-is addendums
      6. Winterization
      7. Preapproval with selling lender
      8. Selection of title company
