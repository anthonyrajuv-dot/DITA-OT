Topic 2.1: The Science and Art of Manager Selection

Manager Performance and Persistence

Investors often hire managers to choose asset allocations and implement investment strategies. Mutual fund, hedge fund, and private equity fund managers are often chosen based on historical performance. Research by Barclays on excess returns of the top 25% (i.e., top quartile) of managers and peer group medians for relevant benchmarks from 2005 to 2014 suggests that top-quartile manager performance is strong but volatile.

Performance persistence tables indicate whether manager performance persists over time. Mutual fund large- and mid-cap performance persistence data indicate that:

  • Top-quartile managers generally do not continue to perform in the top quartile in subsequent periods.
  • The percentage of top-quartile managers that remains in subsequent periods drops over time.

This evidence supports the old adage that “past performance is not a good indicator of future performance.”

The goal of manager selection is to identify managers who have performed well over a period as a result of manager skill, not good luck. Success is defined differently, depending on the type of fund. For long-only funds a successful manager may:

  • Add alpha.
  • Exceed a target risk-adjusted excess return relative to a low-cost index alternative (i.e., traditional investments).

The definition of success is adjusted when no low-cost index alternative exists. It may be defined by a:

  • Positive absolute return over the market cycle or life of the investment.
  • Return greater than an available liquid substitute.
  • Risk-adjusted return greater than published peer group returns investing in similar strategies over similar periods.

Investment Due Diligence

The four parts to the investment due diligence process are:

  1. Quantitative screening. Quantitative screening uses a limited number of metrics to filter down the very large pool of investment managers to a relevant set of skilled managers.
  2. Investment team and organizational attributes.
    • Examine the manager’s past performance.
    • Identify manager attitudes towards risk and identify investment decision-making processes.
    • Conduct face-to-face interviews.
    • Compare actual performance and risk taking with manager narratives about performance and risk taking.
    • Analyze the firm’s organizational structure.
    • Evaluate compensation structures.
    • Evaluate asset size and growth.
    • Evaluate the time it takes to liquidate assets (e.g., for private equity, how difficult is it to sell?).
  3. Investment process review.
    • Does the manager have a comparative advantage with respect to collecting, analyzing, and choosing investments?
    • How does the manager use information to his advantage?
    • Does the formal investment process contribute to the manager’s ability to perform? Do guidelines check emotional decision making?
  4. Performance analysis. A manager’s historical performance must be examined and should be compared to an appropriate benchmark. Performance measures include:
    • Risk-adjusted (i.e., excess) returns.
    • Beta.
    • Alpha.
    • Standard deviation of returns.
    • Sharpe ratio, Jensen’s alpha, information ratio, up/down capture ratio, and consistency ratios.
    • Risk/reward.
    • Correlation with other assets.
    • Individual IRRs.
    • Multiple of capital generated by the investment.
    • The distribution of profits. That is, are there a few big wins, or many, more moderate wins (more replicable)?
    • The level of risk taking and the magnitude of the largest drawdown.

Investors should prevent losses due to operational oversights and errors by performing operational due diligence. The four main steps in the operational due diligence process are:

  1. Document and legal review. Limited partnership agreements, subscription documents, and private placement memorandum must be examined.
  2. Verification of vendor relationships. Verify outside relationships and confirm the services they provide, such as independent NAV calculations.
  3. Background checks on portfolio managers and senior members of the firm.
  4. On-site visits. Vendor relationships, accounting functions, cyber security, compliance procedures, and governance may be reviewed and discussed.

While turnover is costly, managers must be terminated in some cases. Resignations or firings of key employees, excessive (i.e., unsustainable) asset growth, negative changes in the ownership structure of the firm, unexpected changes in the firm’s investment processes, worsening compliance procedures, deteriorating performance, or deteriorating liquidity should all raise red flags and possibly lead to manager termination.
