Valuation Risks in Distressed Debt Investing

LO 29.11: Demonstrate knowledge of the valuation risks involved in distressed debt investing.

For example:

  • Identify factors to consider in valuing a potential distressed debt investment.
  • Discuss the effect of liquidity risk on the value of a distressed debt investment.
  • Discuss the effect of mark-to-market risk on the value of a distressed debt investment.
  • Discuss the effect of legal and jurisdiction risk on the value of a distressed debt investment.

Assessing the value of a distressed entity and its debt requires investors to make a set of assumptions. Investors must not only be familiar with the financial metrics of the distressed entity, but also need to make projections for a series of downside probabilities. For example, a distressed borrower’s working capital needs could rise if suppliers cease to extend further credit to the entity.

Liquidity risk is a common concern for distressed investing. Thinly traded securities typically trade at a wide bid-ask spread, with spreads of 2 to 4 basis points common for bonds and 10 to 20 basis points for loans.

Reduced liquidity also causes large volatility swings in prices and significant mark-to-market value changes.

It is paramount that investors in distressed securities are familiar with international bankruptcy laws and regulations. This includes a familiarity with historical court cases and rulings and the preferences of specific judges. As a result, investors often specialize in a particular jurisdiction to avoid the uncertainty associated with investing in jurisdictions where they lack familiarity. Investors should also be aware of forum shopping, a scenario where a debtor would file in a bankruptcy court that it expects will rule most favorably. In this scenario, the investor’s familiarity with the history of the judge’s ruling helps anticipate the outcome.
