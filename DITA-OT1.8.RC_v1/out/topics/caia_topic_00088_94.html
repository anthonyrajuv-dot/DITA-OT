Leveraged and Option-Based Structures

LO 24.3: Demonstrate knowledge of leveraged and option-based commodity investment structures.

For example:

  • Describe leveraged and short commodity index-based products, and apply the mathematics inherent in understanding daily performance calculations.
  • Describe how leveraged notes may offer leveraged exposure to commodity indices.
  • Discuss the characteristics of principal-guaranteed notes.

Leveraged commodity investments include leveraged ETFs and leveraged notes including ETNs. These investments allow investors to magnify either their short or long commodity exposure using leverage. However, because leveraged ETFs and ETNs provide daily leverage, long-term leveraged returns deviate substantially from the leveraged underlying return. This is because the extra volatility associated with these leveraged instruments affects the return. Therefore, investors who use these investments on a long-term basis will incur substantial transaction costs due to the continual rebalancing needed to earn returns that equal the long-term index return multiplied by the leverage level.

Leveraged notes are index-linked notes that offer multiples of exposure to the commodity index (e.g., three times exposure would allow a $1,000 investment to control $3,000 of commodity exposure). Leveraged notes have an embedded put option (i.e., notes have limited liability). If the index declines more than 33% in value, the note would default, but the investor would be protected via an embedded put option. The put cost is included in the up-front fees for the investment. The primary advantage of this structure is high commodity exposure with only a moderate up-front commitment.

Principal-guaranteed notes (also known as principal-protected notes) participate in commodity index increases, but the principal is protected from index declines. This structure is popular with retail, but not institutional investors. Note that the principal protection offered by these notes is subject to the creditworthiness of the issuer. Two common structures include:

  • Cash and call strategy (also known as a participation note). In this structure, the commodity returns are earned through call options, while principal returns are earned through zero-coupon notes. For example, a three-year note may consist of buying a $900 zero-coupon bond that will be worth $1,000 at its maturity in three years and using the remaining $100 to purchase call options on a particular commodity index. If the call options expire worthless, the investor still receives their $1,000, but if the commodity index appreciates and the call options become valuable, the investor will earn more than $1,000.
  • Dynamic strategy (also known as constant proportion portfolio insurance). In this structure, investor capital is invested in commodities and zero-coupon bonds (which forms an investment floor) based on a pre-specified proportion. As the commodities’ values change, the investment’s allocation is rebalanced. If commodities increase (decrease) in value, the commodity allocation increases (decreases). In the event of a steep decline for commodities, the investment would be 100% in bonds, and investors would be unable to participate in a future commodity price increase during the remaining life of the investment.
