Topic 7.9: Hedge Fund Operational Due Diligence

Distinguishing Private Equity and Hedge Fund Operational Due Diligence

Hedge funds and private equity funds have substantial differences that affect operational due diligence (ODD) processes because:

  • Hedge funds trade their positions more frequently.
  • Hedge funds have holdings that are more liquid.

Various hedge funds may hold positions that are illiquid and are considered private equity hybrids, so be aware that there is a continuum of strategies between hedge funds and private equity.

Operational Steps

The four operational steps of the hedge fund trading process are:

  1. Execution. Process of completing a trade.
  2. Posting and settlement. Internal logging of trades (posting) and completing the trade confirmations (settlement).
  3. Allocation. Process of distributing new positions among funds.
  4. Reconciliation. Internal review that ensures fund’s trade details match those of the counterparty. Investors conducting ODD should ensure that any issues raised by third parties are resolved without influence from the hedge fund.

Cash Management and Movement

The way hedge funds move and account for cash is an important ODD matter. Hedge funds have four primary categories of cash:

  1. Cash for fund expenses.
  2. Cash for trading.
  3. Cash flow from and to investors.
  4. Unencumbered cash.

Internal hedge fund reviews and administrator checks are critical to:

  • Ensure that subscriptions are placed in the correct funds.
  • Monitor the process to ensure payments go to the right people in the correct amount.
  • Evaluate timing of redemptions and subscriptions to ensure compliance with fund terms and guidelines.
  • Review redemption and subscription documents for compliance with accredited investor and anti–money laundering regulations.

External Parties

Hedge fund prime brokers facilitate hedge fund trading by aggregating a hedge fund’s securities and cash while also providing short selling and leverage capabilities. Hedge fund investors must perform ODD on prime brokers to ensure that they can stay solvent through a financial crisis.

Nearly all hedge funds use hedge fund administrators to perform fund accounting and shareholder services as it is a best practice to have a third party involved in the fund valuation process. The timing of when the fund’s net asset value (NAV) is released is a key consideration during the ODD process. A delayed NAV is an operational signaling effect that may be indicative of undiagnosed operational issues between a hedge fund and an administrator.

An investor should have an independent service provider conduct both asset verification and position verification before investing in a hedge fund and at regular intervals after investment.

Compliance Considerations of Hedge Funds

Compliance responsibilities at hedge funds include:

  • Personnel training on compliance-related matters on a regular basis.
  • Testing and implementation of compliance policies.
  • Managing and monitoring conflicts of interest.

Other areas of focus include:

  • Personal trading. Hedge funds typically monitor trading in the personal accounts of their employees, the employees’ spouses, and other immediate family members to avoid front running, or trades made ahead of a firm’s clients. This is a practice that disadvantages the firm’s investors.
  • Insider trading. Many high-profile insider trading cases have involved the use of expert network firms that provide primary research to buy-side clients by providing access to industry experts and arranging phone calls and meetings.
  • Electronic communication monitoring. Compliance departments can take several steps to implement better controls of expert networks. Investors should understand how a hedge fund facilitates communications of its daily operational and investment procedures. Email, instant message, and other electronic methods must be properly monitored and archived when required so that transmission of material nonpublic information can be prevented.
  • Third-party compliance consultants. Many firms work with consultants to perform the following tasks:
    • Performing mock regulatory audits.
    • Updating compliance procedures and policies to meet new regulations.
    • Running compliance testing programs.
    • Providing compliance expertise to satellite offices with no compliance personnel.

Documenting Operational Due Diligence Processes

Process documentation refers to the creation of a document that details the conclusions and results of an ODD investigation. Best practice is to create a document with an executive summary, a list of strengths, and areas of operational risk.

Allocation Considerations and Operational Decision Making

At the end of the ODD process, an operational-risk determination must be made. If the investor determines that the operational risk of the fund is unacceptably high, the investor may decline to invest in the fund or may reevaluate the fund after the fund’s deficiencies have been fixed. Conversely, an investor will make an investment allocation if the fund’s operational risk is sufficiently low. Comparing operational best practices to existing procedures at a fund is known as operational benchmarking.

Investigative Due Diligence

The three models used for investigative due diligence include:

  1. Equity ownership model. Investigations are performed on all equity owners. Only suitable for small funds with few owners.
  2. Investment decision-making authority model. Portfolio managers, traders, and other persons with investment authority are investigated.
  3. Risk control model. Background investigations are performed on both noninvestment and investment personnel who control risk at a fund.

Background investigations commonly include five areas:

  • Criminal searches. Arrest records, criminal convictions, and traffic violations.
  • Civil searches. All civil litigation including bankruptcies, foreclosures, liens, and tax cases.
  • Regulatory searches. Regulatory records including U.S. SEC, U.K. FCA, and any hedge fund filings.
  • Media searches. Internet, social media, and news sources.
  • Factual information searches. Education and employment verification, reference checks, and outside business interests.

Interpreting the results of an investigation is often a subjective matter because of limited information and investors’ varying tolerances of red flags that arise during an investigation.

Resource Allocation

When conducting ODD reviews, investors typically select one of four approaches.

  1. Dedicated. Full-time employee examines operational risks of hedge fund managers.
  2. Shared. Same team conducts operational and investment due diligence.
  3. Modular. Divide process and have specialists handle each functional component.
  4. Hybrid. Combination of the dedicated, shared, and modular approaches.

Hedge Fund Governance

Fund governance refers to the interconnected system of procedures and controls that determine fund transparency, independence, and oversight. Five common operations-related committees for hedge funds are:

  • Operations committee.
  • Valuation committee.
  • Disaster recovery and business continuity committee.
  • Best execution committee.
  • Compliance committee.

A board of directors is responsible for providing oversight of the funds and fulfilling the fund’s legal rights and regulatory obligations. Directors are responsible for providing oversight of the staff that executes the day-to-day operations of the fund.

Hedge Fund Insurance

Insurance coverage can provide important risk protection to the hedge fund, its service providers, and investors. Investors should investigate whether the amounts and types of insurance a hedge fund has are appropriate. Common types of insurance coverage by hedge funds include:

  • Directors’ and officers’ liability coverage (D&O).
  • Errors and omissions (E&O).
  • Employment practices liability coverage.
  • General partner liability coverage.
  • Key-person insurance.
  • Property and liability coverage.

Investors also should be aware of the insurance coverage at a hedge fund’s service providers.

Fund of Hedge Funds Operational Due Diligence

An FoF manager allocates capital and, unlike a hedge fund, does not typically execute trades. Best practice is to evaluate the quality of a hedge fund-of-fund’s ODD because it needs to be investigating and evaluating the underlying funds to which it allocates. FoF investors also should be aware of their underlying funds’ redemption and lockup periods so that potential liquidity mismatches are identified.
