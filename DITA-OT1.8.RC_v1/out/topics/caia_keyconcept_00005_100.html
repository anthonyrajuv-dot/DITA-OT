LO 29.9

Companies can restructure their debt obligations either through an out-of-court restructuring or through bankruptcy. Out-of-court restructuring involves a consensual approach between creditors and borrowers in order to reduce the debt burden.

The bankruptcy laws of both the United States and the United Kingdom are considered creditor friendly:

  • United States. A bankruptcy can either follow a Chapter 11 (reorganization) or Chapter 7 bankruptcy (liquidation), and can be filed either on a voluntarily or involuntarily basis. Under a voluntary filing, the debtor, known as the debtor-in-possession (DIP), retains control of the restructuring process and typically presents a prepackaged filing (prepack) that includes a plan of reorganization (POR). Valuation is derived primarily from comparable sales and objective valuation and aims to ensure the debtor remains a going concern.
  • United Kingdom. A restructuring may involve scheme of arrangement used for both reorganizations of solvent companies and insolvent restructurings. Schemes must receive at least 75% approval by each class of creditor. Under administration of restructuring, control is transferred from the debtor to the administrator. Valuation is generally derived from liquidation, which then determines the creditorsâ€™ recovery.

International bankruptcy laws vary significantly and may be considered significantly less debtor or creditor friendly than the United States or the United Kingdom.
