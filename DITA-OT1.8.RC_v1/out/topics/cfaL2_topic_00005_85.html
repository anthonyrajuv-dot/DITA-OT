LOS 54.b: Calculate and interpret the information ratio (ex post and ex ante) and contrast it to the Sharpe ratio.

CFA® Program Curriculum, Volume 6, page 419

The information ratio and the Sharpe ratio are two different methods of measuring a portfolio’s risk-adjusted rate of return.

The Sharpe ratio (SR) is calculated as excess return per unit of risk (standard deviation):

An important attribute of the Sharpe ratio is that it is unaffected by the addition of cash or leverage in the portfolio. A 50% allocation to the risk-free asset would reduce both the excess return and standard deviation of returns by half.

The information ratio (IR) is the ratio of the active return to the standard deviation of active returns, which is known as active risk:

Some important points:

  • In this topic review, the information ratio that is discussed is ex-ante or based on expectations. The ex-ante information ratio is always positive (otherwise active management is not prudent), while for ex-post, the information ratio can be negative.
  • A closet index fund is a fund that claims to be actively managed but actually closely tracks the underlying benchmark index. These funds tend to have the same (or very close) Sharpe ratio as the benchmark index and a very low information ratio and little active risk. After fees, information ratio of closet index funds is often negative.
  • A fund with zero systematic risk (e.g., a market-neutral long-short equity fund) that has a risk-free rate as its benchmark would have an identical information ratio and Sharpe ratio.
  • Unlike the Sharpe ratio, the information ratio is altered by the addition of cash or the use of leverage.
  • The information ratio of an unconstrained portfolio is unaffected by the aggressiveness of the active weights. If the active weights of a portfolio are tripled, the active return and the active risk both triple so the information ratio unchanged.
  • The information ratio of a portfolio that combines the actively managed portfolio of an allocation to the benchmark portfolio will be the same as the information ratio of the actively managed portfolio. This results from the fact that as we increase the weight of the benchmark portfolio, the active return and active risk decrease proportionately, leaving the information ratio unchanged.
  • Investors can take appropriate active risk by investing a portion of their portfolio in the active portfolio and the remaining portion in the benchmark. For example, if the active risk of a fund is 10%, an investor seeking to limit active risk to 6% can do so by investing 60% in the active portfolio and remaining 40% in the benchmark portfolio.
