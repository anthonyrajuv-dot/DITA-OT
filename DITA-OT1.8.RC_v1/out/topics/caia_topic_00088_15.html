  • Describe the relationship between the variances of true and reported returns.

Note that the mean returns are somewhat similar between the two indices but that the standard deviation of the smoothed NPI is substantially lower. Also, the NPI is unleveraged while the NAREIT Index reflects leverage. Leverage magnifies volatility.

The NAREIT Index experienced large negative returns at the end of 2008 and beginning of 2009. However, the NPI experienced much smaller negative returns during the same period. This supports the notion that indices that use appraised values only partially respond to market changes. The NAREIT Index rebounds in the next two quarters, but the NPI continues to fall, reflecting the lagged response to the previous decline. However, investors find it difficult to profit from the lag due to the inability to short sell private funds. Also, open-end funds often cease or slow redemptions during periods of turmoil to prevent short-term trading.

Using the lagged (i.e., one period prior) returns, the REIT first-order autocorrelation coefficient is 21.34% and the NPI first-order autocorrelation coefficient is 84.48%. As expected, the correlation with previous returns is much higher for the appraisal (i.e., smoothed) data than for the market-based returns. The financial crisis that began in 2007 is reflected in this data, so outliers may have unduly influenced the autocorrelation coefficients.

Using the estimated rhos () and the lagged return values, it is possible to unsmooth the entire return series for both indices.

Note that in Figure 2, as expected, the standard deviation of the unsmoothed NPI is higher than the smoothed returns (11.36% versus 3.27%). Because there was some evidence of autocorrelation (i.e.,  = 21.34%) in the market-based NAREIT Index, the standard deviation of returns increased slightly as well from 13.73%% to 17.34%. The correlation could be higher during this period as a result of turbulent financial markets.

The initial NPI standard deviation increased from approximately 25% of that of the NAREIT Index based on smoothed returns to more than 65% of that of the NAREIT Index based on unsmoothed returns. The NAREIT Index reflects higher risk in both cases, in part due to higher leverage.

One of the key things to notice is that if an investor used a mean/variance optimization approach to allocate resources, the low variance of the NPI would result in an artificially high allocation. Sharpe ratios are higher for smoothed returns. This highlights the importance of unsmoothing returns. The true volatility is higher and thus the allocation would be lower if the investor used unsmoothed returns. Also, even when short-term volatility appears to be low, there is a much higher long-term loss potential in a period of falling prices due to positive autocorrelation.

Smoothed returns result in correlation coefficients with other asset classes that are understated as well. Again, this leads to an overallocation to asset classes with smoothed returns. Unsmoothing the data makes an upward adjustment in both the standard deviation of returns and estimated correlations with other asset classes.

Professor’s Note: There is something called the real estate risk premium puzzle that asks why private equity real estate risk-adjusted returns seem abnormally high relative to other asset classes. The answer may simply be that they are not high, they just appear high due to underestimated volatility and overestimated risk-adjusted return measures, such as the Sharpe ratio. The bottom line is that unsmoothing returns, a somewhat sophisticated technique not used by many real estate analysts, is important because it provides better insights into the true risks and risk-adjusted returns of real estate in a diversified portfolio.
