Topic 2.8: Making Waves: The Cresting Co-Investment Opportunity

Co-Investment Structures

FoF allocations:

  • Generally allocate 5%–15% to co-investments.
  • Fees may be higher than regular FoF investments.

Multi-sponsor funds:

  • Managed by a sponsor with access to many general partners (GPs).
  • LPs invest in a diverse set of co-investment opportunities with multiple GPs.
  • Passive strategy with direct co-investment exposure.
  • May use a lower 1 and 10 fee structure.

Single-sponsor (a.k.a. annex) funds:

  • Managed by a single GP that draws LP capital at its discretion.
  • Capital raised from LPs within the main fund as well as LPs outside the fund.
  • May invest in all deals needing funds or may specialize in specific deal types.
  • Fee structure is generally lower.

Direct co-investments:

  • LPs invest directly with a single GP with no obligation to invest specific amounts.
  • LPs must quickly evaluate co-investments but can structure their own strategy.
  • GPs generally charge a management fee and carried interest.

Risk-Return Profiles of Co-Investment Structures

The risk-return profiles, are a function of the level of control and diversification associated with each co-investment structure. The risk-return profiles are as follows:

  • FoF allocations
    • Downside risk is lower (and there is lower upside potential) because co-investment performance is incorporated into overall fund performance.
    • FoFs that apply a consistent fee structure to all investment types can use co-investments to reduce the typical fee drag of 200–400 basis points. This dampens the J-curve effect.
    • Co-investment allocations within FoFs add risk to FoFs overall.
  • Multi-sponsor funds. These funds achieve risk reduction by investing in multiple co-investments across various portfolio companies and GPs, thus diversifying the strategy.
  • Single-sponsor funds. Multiple co-investments increase diversification and reduce risk across various portfolio companies. Single-sponsor funds have less diversification than multi-sponsor funds because only one GP is used to source co-investments.
  • Direct co-investments. Investing directly with a GP allows an LP the greatest control but generally carries the greatest risk relative to other co-investment structures.

Benefits of Co-Investment

Analyzing co-investment performance is difficult because return data is incomplete and fragmented. Co-investments benefit LPs in terms of:

  • A favorable risk-return profile.
  • J-curve mitigation.
  • Higher efficiency of capital deployment.
  • The ability to tailor portfolios.
  • Increased investment skill. However, analyzing co-investment performance and the benefits to investors is problematic as return data is incomplete and fragmented.

Challenges of Co-Investment

To create a successful co-investment program, several factors must be in place:

  • Unique skillset. A unique skillset is required (must understand portfolio companies and their industries/environments).
  • Sourcing opportunities. Investors must have the ability to source opportunities efficiently, which means a steady stream of co-investment opportunities is necessary.
  • Timing. The ability to appropriately time investments, which is dependent on deal flow.
  • Decision-making process. Investors must have a clear decision-making process that can happen quickly and sometimes with incomplete information as LPs have a short time period (e.g., two weeks) to evaluate deals.
  • Adverse selection. Co-investments exhibit a wide range of performance. GPs may offer less attractive opportunities. However, GPs generally offer co-investments for legitimate reasons and are concerned about their reputations and abilities to raise future funds, which may mitigate the adverse selection risk.
  • Benchmark. Co-investments do not have a clearly defined benchmark that can be applied across all co-investments. Potential, although imperfect, benchmarks include traditional private equity funds in a given vintage year or all co-investments within a given vintage year. The best co-investment benchmark is the gross returns of all private equity investments within a given calendar year, which represents the opportunity set of deals before fees (which can vary by investment).

Improving Co-Investment Outcomes

To improve the co-investment outcomes, investors must address the above challenges. LPs need to be aware of opportunities to increase the likelihood of a successful program. Investors must account for unique risks, determine the right balance of internal and external resources, and find methods to avoid adverse selection.
